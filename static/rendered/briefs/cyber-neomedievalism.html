Working title

Overlapping authority in cyberspace: neo-medieval order, panoptic infrastructures, and chokepoint systems such as SWIFT

Why I am writing this

Most of my public work so far is technical: malware triage, tooling, and CTI-style reports. I also have graduate training in strategic studies. This brief is an attempt to join those lanes without turning cyber into loose metaphor.

The question I want to answer carefully:

Does contemporary cyberspace exhibit features of what Hedley Bull called a neo-medieval pattern of order, and if so, how do panoptic infrastructures and financial messaging systems like SWIFT illuminate that claim for middle-power security practice?

Provisional thesis

Cyberspace does not abolish the state system. It does intensify overlapping authorities: platforms, standards bodies, private coercive actors, alliance networks, and critical infrastructures that sit across borders. Some of those infrastructures function less like open markets and more like panoptic chokepoints: systems that make activity visible to a privileged observer and costly to exit. SWIFT is one of the clearest non-cyber analogies, and several cyber/technical systems rhyme with it.

If that is right, the practical issue for a country like New Zealand is not romantic “digital feudalism.” It is how to do information assurance and cyber defence inside authorities you do not fully own.

Concept map (draft)

1. Hedley Bull and neo-medievalism

Bull’s neo-medievalism points to a possible pattern of international order with:

  • overlapping authorities rather than a single monopolistic sovereign
  • multiple loyalties and sources of law
  • private as well as public organised force
  • durable order without classic Westphalian simplicity

I am not claiming Bull predicted the internet. I am testing whether parts of cyber governance look more neo-medieval than purely inter-state.

Likely evidence buckets:

  • platform jurisdiction over users and content
  • ransomware / access-broker markets as private coercion
  • polycentric technical governance (DNS, standards, CERT networks)
  • cloud and identity providers as de facto landlords of operational life

Counter-case I must keep front and centre: states still dominate sanctions, extradition, military cyber, licensing, and much attribution. Overlap can exist inside a state system, not instead of it.

2. The panopticon

Bentham’s panopticon (and Foucault’s later reading) is about asymmetric visibility: the few can observe the many, behaviour changes under possible observation, and architecture does political work.

In cyber and adjacent infrastructures, panoptic features appear when:

  • a central rail can log, delay, or deny participation
  • exit is expensive or impractical
  • visibility is unequal (operator sees more than participant)
  • compliance becomes the price of remaining in the system

This is useful because it stops the neo-medieval claim from floating. Overlapping authority is abstract. A panoptic rail is concrete.

3. SWIFT as a bridging case

SWIFT is not “the internet.” It is a cooperative financial messaging infrastructure with:

  • extraordinary network effects
  • high switching costs
  • privileged visibility and governance for participants and overseers
  • demonstrated use as a geopolitical instrument (access, exclusion, pressure)

I want to use SWIFT as a comparative case, not a cyber example pretending to be one:

Feature SWIFT-like chokepoint Cyber / tech analogue (candidates)
Hard to exit Membership / correspondent dependence Major cloud, mobile OS, app stores, certificate ecosystems
Asymmetric visibility Message metadata and compliance rails Platform telemetry, CDN logs, identity providers
Governance beyond one state Cooperative + political pressure ICANN/IANA, root/DNS politics, hyperscaler ToS + local law
Coercive leverage Exclusion / restricted access Account bans, cert distrust, upstream filtering, payment-rail cutoffs

The analytic gain is this: neo-medieval overlap describes the shape of authority. Panoptic chokepoints describe how some authorities discipline behaviour without classic territorial monopoly.

What I think the “so what” might become

For NZ / middle-power practice (still drafting):

  1. Sovereign control of the full stack is often fantasy. Assurance under fragmented authority is the real craft.
  2. Dependence on panoptic rails (cloud, identity, update channels, payment and messaging adjacencies) is a strategic fact, not only a procurement detail.
  3. Defensive capacity should assume partial visibility and partial control: detection, resilience, trusted supplier management, alliance sharing.
  4. Analytic products should separate metaphor from institution. If a claim cannot name an organisation, incentive, or exit cost, it is not ready.
