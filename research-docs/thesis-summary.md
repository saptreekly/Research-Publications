# 5th-Generation Fighter Exports as Strategic Competition: How China is Winning the Middle-Power Game in Asia-Pacific

My Master's thesis at Victoria University of Wellington examined this topic in depth, but the original document isn't something I have easy access to anymore. Given how much has shifted in APAC's security landscape since I wrote it, I figured a fresh piece was more useful than a summary of a work that is a bit outdated. Consider this an unpdated, condensed version.

## Intro

100 words

Open with the core tension: the US build the most advanced fighter aircraft in history and turned it into a strategic liability. Introduce the central argument -- that export controls designed as leverage have become a vulnerability China is systematically exploiting across APAC middle-powers.

## I. The F-35 Paradox

~150 words

- The US assumption that superior capability creates dependency and leverage
- How that logic inverts when allies have alternatives
- The Turkiye/Erdoğan case -- a NATO ally using F-35 access as reverse leverage, blocking the sale to extract concessions
- the broader pattern: US export controls create negotiating chips for the other side, not just Washington

## II. China's FC-31 and the Lockheed Martin Breach

~150 words

- Brief OSINT-based capability assessment of the FC-31/J-35 -- acknowledge the intelligence gap honestly, it might actually strengthen credibility
- The 2015 Lockheed Martin breach and what Chinese actors are assessed to have acquired
- The capability gap between F-35 and FC-31 at time of writing and why that gap may matter less than it appears
- Pakistan as the first FC-31 export customer -- what that signals about China's intent

## III. Middle-Power Calculus

~200 words

- Why middle-powers in APAC -- Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, Pakistan, etc. -- face a structurally different choice than US allies in Europe
- The US acquisition path: congressional approval, export licensing, conditions, political strings
- The Chinese acquisition path: faster, cheaper, fewer conditions, comes with infrastructure investment and diplomatic alignment
- This isn't just about aircraft, it's about which great power a middle-powers want to be strucurally dependent on
- The security dilemma dynamics: as China places more advaned platforms in the region, middle-powers feel pressure to acquire capability, which accelerates the competition

## IV. What this means for APAC's Security Architecture

- The long-term trajectory: a bifurcated APAC where some states operate US-ecosystem platforms and others operate Chinese-ecosystem platforms
- Interoperability implications for Five Eyes and Quad partners
- The intelligence dimensions -- every FC-31 sale is also a sensor network, a maintenance relationship, a supply chain dependency
- Why this matters for threat intelligence: understanding the platform tells you about the alliance, the doctrine, and the threat actor's operational ceiling.

## Conclusion

- China isn't winning this competition through superior technology
- It's winning through superior strategic patient and a bettr offer for states that don't want to be caught between great powers
- The US export control aparatus was designed for a world where there was no credible alternative -- that world no longer exists in APAC
- Close with the analytical implication: tracing FC-31 proliferation is now a leading indicator for APAC alignment shifts.
