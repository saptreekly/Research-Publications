# 5th-Generation Fighter Exports as Strategic Competition in Asia-Pacific

My Master's thesis at Victoria University of Wellington examined this topic in depth, but the original document isn't something I have easy access to anymore. Given how much has shifted in APAC's security landscape since I wrote it, a fresh piece seemed more useful than a summary of work that is now partly outdated. Consider this an updated, condensed version of the core argument.

## Introduction

The United States built the most advanced fighter aircraft in history and, in doing so, created a strategic paradox. Export controls and alliance conditionality were meant to convert technological superiority into leverage. Access to the F-35 ecosystem would bind partners closer to Washington and constrain adversaries. Across much of Asia-Pacific, that logic is inverting. Middle powers now face a credible Chinese alternative in the fifth-generation export market, and the very restrictions designed to preserve US advantage are becoming negotiating leverage for the other side. In APAC's middle-power game, export policy has become a vulnerability China is systematically exploiting.

## I. The F-35 Paradox

American strategy assumed that capability gaps create dependency. If only Washington could supply a true fifth-generation platform, allies and partners would accept political strings, congressional scrutiny, and long acquisition timelines as the price of relevance.

That assumption breaks when buyers have alternatives. Export controls no longer function as one-way leverage. They become bargaining chips held by the purchaser as well as the seller. Türkiye under Erdoğan illustrated the pattern inside NATO itself. Ankara's participation in the F-35 programme gave it reverse leverage over Washington, using threatened disruption of the supply chain and basing arrangements to extract concessions rather than accepting them. The broader lesson extends beyond any single ally's politics. When access to a platform is conditional, the condition cuts both ways, and middle powers know it.

## II. China's FC-31 and the Lockheed Martin Breach

China's export offering centres on the FC-31 (J-35) programme. Open-source assessment still leaves significant gaps in verified performance data, and acknowledging that gap matters analytically. Overstating Chinese capability is as dangerous as dismissing it. What is clearer is the industrial and intelligence trajectory behind the platform. The 2015 Lockheed Martin breach, attributed to Chinese actors, is widely assessed to have accelerated access to design and manufacturing knowledge relevant to low-observable aviation. Parity with the F-35 on every metric remains unlikely. At time of writing the capability gap remained real. Even so, the gap may matter less than Washington assumes for buyers whose requirement is regional deterrence and political flexibility rather than full-spectrum NATO interoperability.

Pakistan's selection as an early FC-31 export customer is equally informative. It signals that Beijing is willing to move faster and with fewer political preconditions than the US export regime allows, and to use defence sales as the leading edge of a wider alignment package.

## III. Middle-Power Calculus

Middle powers in APAC (Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, Pakistan, and others) do not face the same choice set as US treaty allies in Europe. Geography, trade dependence, and domestic politics pull simultaneously toward Washington and Beijing. Aircraft acquisition sits at the intersection of those pressures.

The US path is familiar. It involves congressional notification, export licensing, end-use monitoring, and the implicit requirement to fit into a US-centric maintenance, training, and data-sharing architecture. That path is credible, interoperable with Five Eyes and Quad partners, and politically costly. The Chinese path is typically faster, cheaper, and bundled with infrastructure finance, diplomatic support, and fewer visible conditions. Buyers usually weigh which great power they are willing to depend on for decades of sustainment, upgrades, and spare parts. Pure performance comparisons matter less than that structural choice.

Reliability weighs on the same scale. ASEAN capitals watched the Obama administration's rebalance to Asia produce less sustained engagement than many regional leaders had expected. Under Trump, tariffs, trade restrictions, and broader economic pressure became routine tools of coercive statecraft, applied at times against partners as well as rivals. Security commitments grew harder to read when they sat alongside unpredictable economic coercion. Middle powers deciding on a US platform ecosystem are also deciding whether Washington will honour access agreements across political transitions, remain active in regional diplomacy, and treat economic and security partnerships as separate tracks. Procurement timelines run thirty years or more. That record is part of the calculation.

That dependency carries security-dilemma dynamics. As more advanced Chinese platforms enter the region, neighbours feel pressure to respond, either by acquiring capability themselves or by deepening ties with the US. Each purchase accelerates bifurcation. Middle powers shape great-power competition through procurement choices rather than merely absorbing it.

## IV. What This Means for APAC's Security Architecture

The likely trajectory is a bifurcated region. One cluster of states will operate US-ecosystem platforms with shared datalinks, maintenance standards, and intelligence arrangements. Another will operate Chinese-ecosystem platforms with parallel supply chains and training relationships. Interoperability within each cluster may deepen while cross-cluster cooperation becomes harder to sustain in a crisis.

Every FC-31 sale is also an intelligence problem. A fighter is a sensor network, a maintenance relationship, a software update channel, and a long-term industrial dependency. Platform choice reveals alignment intent, operational doctrine, and the ceiling of what a partner can contribute in a contingency. For threat intelligence and strategic analysis, tracking fifth-generation export campaigns is a leading indicator of alignment shifts, not merely an order-of-battle update.

## Conclusion

China is winning this competition through strategic patience and a better-tailored offer for states that refuse to be trapped between great powers on Washington's timeline alone. Superior technology alone does not explain the trend. The US export-control apparatus was designed for a world without a credible fifth-generation alternative in APAC. That world no longer exists.

FC-31 proliferation should be monitored as an early signal of alignment shifts. For middle powers, the choice of ecosystem is coercive statecraft in hardware form. For analysts, it is one of the clearest windows available into how APAC's security architecture is being redrawn.
